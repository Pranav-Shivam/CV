📚 Theory — What is the Multi-Armed Bandit Problem?
▼

🎰 Multi-Armed Bandit

Imagine a row of slot machines (one-armed bandits). Each machine gives a different average reward. You don't know which is best. You must choose wisely to maximize your total reward over time.

🔍 Exploration

Trying different arms to discover their rewards. Without exploration, you might get stuck with a suboptimal choice. Exploration gathers information.

💡 Exploitation

Using what you already know — choosing the arm with the highest estimated reward. Exploitation maximizes reward based on current knowledge.

⚖️ The Dilemma

You can't fully explore AND fully exploit at the same time. This is the explore-exploit tradeoff — one of the most fundamental problems in reinforcement learning.

ε-Greedy Strategy

With probability ε, explore (pick randomly). With probability 1−ε, exploit (pick the best known arm). ε = 0.1 means 10% exploration, 90% exploitation.

Q(a) — Action Value

Q(a) is your estimate of how good action a is. It equals the average reward received whenever you chose action a. Higher Q = better estimated action.

N(a) — Visit Count

N(a) is how many times you've selected action a. More visits = more reliable estimate of Q(a). Initially N(a) = 0 for all actions.

α — Step Size (Alpha)

Alpha controls how fast Q-values update. With α = 1/N (sample average), old and new data are weighted equally. With constant α, recent rewards matter more — useful in non-stationary problems.

❌ Why Pure Greedy Fails

If ε = 0, you only exploit. If you got lucky early with a bad arm, you'll keep choosing it forever. You never discover better arms. Exploration breaks this trap.
